SOURCE: quality_assurance.txt

---

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW

The quality of our work is our reputation. Every deliverable that leaves our organisation and goes to a client must meet a high standard of accuracy, clarity, and professionalism. This policy sets out the quality assurance process for all client deliverables.

---

SECTION 2: THE PEER REVIEW PROCESS

What requires a peer review: All client-facing deliverables require a peer review before being shared with the client. This includes reports, presentations, proposals, financial models, process maps, and any other document prepared for client consumption. Internal working documents do not require a formal peer review but should still be reviewed by the engagement manager.

Who conducts the review: The peer reviewer must be someone not directly involved in producing the deliverable. For standard deliverables, any consultant at the same level or above may conduct the review. For high-stakes deliverables (final reports, proposals over £100,000, board-level presentations), the reviewer must be at Principal level or above.

Review timeline: Build peer review time into your project plan. The reviewer must be given at least 2 business days to complete their review. Do not ask a reviewer to turn something around in less than 24 hours — if this is unavoidable due to a client deadline, escalate to your engagement manager.

---

SECTION 3: WHAT REVIEWERS CHECK

Reviewers should assess the following dimensions:

Accuracy: Are all facts, figures, and data points correct and properly sourced? Are calculations correct? Are any claims that could be challenged by the client properly evidenced?

Logic and structure: Does the document tell a clear, logical story? Is the structure appropriate for the audience? Does the executive summary accurately reflect the body of the document?

Clarity: Is the writing clear and free of jargon? Would a senior client executive understand this document without additional context? Are charts and visuals clearly labelled and easy to interpret?

Completeness: Does the document address all the questions it is supposed to answer? Are there any gaps or sections that feel underdeveloped?

Presentation: Is the document formatted correctly using the standard template? Are fonts, colours, and spacing consistent? Are there any typos or grammatical errors?

Commercial and legal: Does the document make any commitments, guarantees, or statements that could create commercial or legal risk? If so, flag for Principal review.

---

SECTION 4: THE REVIEW FEEDBACK PROCESS

Feedback must be provided in writing, either as tracked changes in the document or as a written summary of required changes. Verbal-only feedback is not acceptable.

Feedback should be constructive and specific. "This section is unclear" is not sufficient — explain why it is unclear and suggest how to improve it.

The original author is responsible for addressing all feedback before the document is finalised. If the author disagrees with a piece of feedback, they should discuss it with the reviewer. If agreement cannot be reached, escalate to the engagement manager.

---

SECTION 5: QUALITY STANDARDS FOR SPECIFIC DELIVERABLE TYPES

Presentations: Slides should follow the one idea per slide principle. No slide should have more than 5 bullet points. All charts must have a clear title that states the insight, not just the topic (e.g. "Revenue has grown 40% in three years" not "Revenue chart"). Use our standard slide template — no exceptions for client-facing work.

Financial models: All models must be built using the standard modelling template. Inputs, calculations, and outputs must be clearly separated. All assumptions must be documented on a dedicated assumptions tab. Models must be sense-checked against known benchmarks before being shared with the client.

Reports: Follow the Pyramid Principle — lead with the conclusion, then provide supporting evidence. Reports should be written for the senior executive, not the project team. Use plain English. Aim for sentences under 25 words on average.

---

SECTION 6: POST-DELIVERY QUALITY REVIEW

After every engagement closes, the engagement manager must complete a Quality Review Form covering: what went well, what quality issues arose during the engagement, and what process improvements are recommended. These forms are reviewed quarterly by the Quality Committee and used to update our standard processes and templates.
